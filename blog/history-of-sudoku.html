A Brief History of Sudoku: From Switzerland to the World

By Stillwater Puzzle Tools · 7 min read

Sudoku has a Japanese name, was popularized by an American puzzle-maker, took off thanks to a New Zealander, and traces its mathematical roots to Switzerland. For a puzzle about tidy logic, its history is wonderfully tangled.

The mathematical ancestor

The deepest root reaches back to the 18th century and the Swiss mathematician Leonhard Euler, who studied "Latin squares" — grids filled so that each symbol appears exactly once in every row and column. That's the mathematical skeleton beneath every Sudoku. Euler wasn't designing a pastime; he was exploring a pure mathematical idea. But the structure he investigated is precisely what makes Sudoku tick.

The modern puzzle takes shape

The puzzle in its familiar form — a 9×9 grid divided into nine boxes, with some numbers given and the rest to be deduced — appeared in the late 1970s, devised for an American puzzle magazine. At the time it went by a plainer name, something like "Number Place," and it was a quiet, modest feature. It didn't set the world alight. Not yet.

Japan gives it a name

In the 1980s the puzzle crossed to Japan, where a puzzle publisher picked it up and gave it the name that stuck. "Sudoku" is a shortening of a Japanese phrase meaning, roughly, "the digits must be single" — a neat description of the rule that no number repeats in a row, column, or box. In Japan it found a genuinely enthusiastic audience and flourished for years as a beloved domestic puzzle, largely unknown to the wider world.

The accidental global launch

Sudoku's leap to worldwide fame came through a wonderfully unlikely route. A retired judge from New Zealand, charmed by the puzzle, spent years writing a computer program that could generate fresh Sudoku grids on demand. He then persuaded a major British newspaper to start running them in 2004. The timing was perfect. The puzzle caught fire almost overnight.

Within months, Sudoku leapt from one newspaper to seemingly all of them, then around the globe. Books, apps, and championships followed in a rush. A puzzle that had simmered quietly for decades became, in barely a year, a worldwide phenomenon.

Why numbers, with no math?

Part of Sudoku's genius — and a reason for its global reach — is that it uses numbers but requires no arithmetic. The digits 1 through 9 are just convenient symbols; you could play with nine letters or nine colors and nothing would change. That makes Sudoku effortlessly cross-cultural: it needs no shared language, only the universal pleasure of logical deduction. A solver in Tokyo, London, or Lima faces exactly the same puzzle.

A puzzle without borders

It's fitting that a puzzle assembled from Swiss mathematics, an American magazine, a Japanese name, and a New Zealander's software should end up belonging to everyone. Sudoku's scattered, multinational origins mirror what it became: a small daily ritual shared across the entire world, built on nothing but a grid and the satisfaction of figuring it out.
